The Earth Illusion
Make reality feel weird.
Then make it make sense.
Start with a classic that punches instantly: the railway illusion (Ponzo). Watch a measurement bar slide to prove the two lines are the same length—while your brain swears they’re not.
Run the demo Why it works
What your eyes insist: the upper bar is longer. What the comparator proves every few seconds: they’re identical. That mismatch is the illusion.

Why your brain gets fooled

Ponzo Illusion. Your visual system uses context to infer size. The converging rails scream “depth.” In depth, objects farther away cast a smaller retinal image. Your brain compensates—so the upper bar (placed where the rails are closer) is perceived as larger even though both bars are equal.

That’s the Earth Illusion in practice: Truth = Form + Position. The form (two equal bars) stays fixed; the position (context of depth cues) changes the truth you experience.

Precision, not gimmicks

The bars are equal in the SVG source. The green comparator is just a ruler that slides—no stretching, no trick code. You can right‑click → Inspect to verify the widths are identical (width="360").

Try this: cover the rails with your fingers. The illusion weakens or vanishes.

Bonus: Müller‑Lyer, same lesson

Same true length (640). Flip the arrowheads and your brain flips the judgment.
