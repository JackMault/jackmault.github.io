Perceptual Stewardship and Identity Refinement: The MIIR Instrument

Jack Mault

Perceptual Stewardship Lab, Mault Co., Grand Forks, ND, USA

Abstract

This white paper introduces the Perceptual Stewardship Lab and outlines MIIR (Model Instantiation for Identity Refinement) as a practical instrument for clarifying identity, measuring confidence shifts, and anchoring transformation via portraiture. Methods emphasize structured interviewing and comparative plotting while preserving participant agency. Implementation details are intentionally omitted here; readers may request a full copy for methodological specifics and results.

Keywords

identity; perception; active inference; measurement; portraiture; stewardship

1. Introduction

The Perceptual Stewardship Lab (PSL) studies how attention, language, and imagery cohere identity over time. MIIR is the lab’s first field instrument, designed for leaders and creators seeking durable clarity. Rather than proposing an abstract ideal-self, MIIR renders an articulated self-model and measures confidence across claims before and after a structured refinement.

2. Background

MIIR is informed by contemporary work in cognitive science and control theory (e.g., active inference) and by craft practices in portraiture. The approach treats identity as a living control policy: intelligible in language, legible in behavior, and stabilizable through well-chosen anchors.

3. Methods (Overview)

At a high level, MIIR proceeds through: (a) priming to set direction, (b) identity mapping to externalize claims and visualize confidence, (c) targeted refinement to reduce noise and strengthen alignment, and (d) a photographic anchor to consolidate change. Full procedural details, itemization, and statistical notes are available upon request.

4. Findings (Conceptual)

  • Participants reliably report higher clarity and confidence in a sparser identity model.
  • Before/after comparisons offer visible signal for reflective decisions and communication.
  • Portraiture functions as an enduring mnemonic and social affordance for the refined model.

5. Discussion

As an instrument, MIIR complements coaching and therapy by emphasizing measurable articulation and a tangible anchor. PSL’s ongoing work explores generalization, session reliability, and longitudinal effects on decision quality.

6. Limitations

This overview omits implementation specifics by design. Controlled studies and expanded datasets are in progress to estimate effect sizes across populations and contexts.

7. Conclusion

MIIR operationalizes perceptual stewardship: it helps participants speak a truer model of themselves, measure movement, and carry the result forward. Requests for the full paper, including methods and appendices, are welcome.
