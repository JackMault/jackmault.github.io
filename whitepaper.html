Perceptual Stewardship and Identity Refinement: The MIIR Instrument — Extended Abstract

Jack Mault

Perceptual Stewardship Lab, Mault Co., Grand Forks, ND, USA

Extended Abstract

The Perceptual Stewardship Lab (PSL) develops instruments that help people steward attention and articulate identity with measurable clarity. MIIR—Model Instantiation for Identity Refinement—is our first instrument. It combines brief priming, identity mapping, targeted refinement, and a portrait-based anchor. The aim is practical: produce a sparser, truer self-model and show visible pre/post change without leaking proprietary prompts or procedures.

Conceptually, MIIR treats identity as a control policy that can be made explicit in language, visualized as claims with confidence, and stabilized with symbolic anchors. This stance fits with modern views of mind that emphasize prediction and model maintenance under uncertainty (e.g., free-energy/predictive-processing accounts), where clarity reduces surprise and improves decisions. Empirically, adjacent literatures suggest three useful lenses:

  • Structural clarity. Self-concept clarity (SCC) captures whether self-beliefs are clearly and confidently defined and internally consistent; higher SCC predicts better well-being and decision stability. MIIR targets movement toward higher SCC without prescribing content.
  • Narrative coherence. Narrative identity research shows life stories integrate past and future to confer meaning; shifts in narrative themes track mental-health trajectories. MIIR’s mapping step externalizes these themes to make revision tractable.
  • Participatory co-modeling. Participatory/“with-stakeholders” modeling offers procedures for building shared models while managing bias and uncertainty; MIIR borrows this stance to co-author an identity model with the participant rather than imposing one.

MIIR’s anchor is a museum-grade portrait produced after refinement. Portraiture here functions as a mnemonic and social affordance—a compact “token” that helps carry the refined model forward in memory and signaling. We avoid behaviorist token economies (which raise generalization issues when reinforcement ends) and instead use a symbolic anchor chosen for durability and personal salience.
